Scientific Sources & Citations

Evidence-based foundations for GGFit's calculations

GGFit uses well-established, peer-reviewed formulas and guidelines from sports nutrition research to calculate your personalized calorie targets, macronutrient recommendations, and recovery estimates. All formulas and recommendations referenced in the app are sourced from the following publications.

⚠️ Important Disclaimer

GGFit is a fitness tracking tool, not a medical device. The calculations and recommendations provided are based on general population research and are intended for informational purposes only. They are not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always consult a qualified healthcare provider or registered dietitian before making significant changes to your diet or exercise program.

1. Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

Mifflin-St Jeor Equation

GGFit uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation to estimate your Basal Metabolic Rate — the number of calories your body needs at rest. This equation is widely regarded as the most accurate BMR prediction formula for healthy adults.

Males: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Females: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161
Mifflin, M.D., St Jeor, S.T., Hill, L.A., Scott, B.J., Daugherty, S.A., & Koh, Y.O. (1990). "A new predictive equation for resting energy expenditure in healthy individuals." The American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, 51(2), 241–247.
doi:10.1093/ajcn/51.2.241
Frankenfield, D., Roth-Yousey, L., & Compher, C. (2005). "Comparison of predictive equations for resting metabolic rate in healthy nonobese and obese adults: a systematic review." Journal of the American Dietetic Association, 105(5), 775–789.
doi:10.1016/j.jada.2005.02.005
This systematic review validated that the Mifflin-St Jeor equation is the most reliable for estimating BMR.

2. Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)

Activity Level Multipliers

GGFit multiplies your BMR by a physical activity factor to estimate your Total Daily Energy Expenditure. The activity multipliers used are based on the widely referenced PAL (Physical Activity Level) scale:

Sedentary (little or no exercise): BMR × 1.2
Light (1–3 days/week): BMR × 1.375
Moderate (3–5 days/week): BMR × 1.55
Active (6–7 days/week): BMR × 1.725
McArdle, W.D., Katch, F.I., & Katch, V.L. (2015). Exercise Physiology: Nutrition, Energy, and Human Performance (8th ed.). Wolters Kluwer.
Standard reference for PAL factors used in sports science and dietetics.

3. Goal-Based Caloric Adjustments

GGFit adjusts your TDEE based on your selected fitness goal:

  • Fat Loss (Cut): TDEE − 500 kcal/day (≈ 0.45 kg / 1 lb per week)
  • Muscle Gain (Bulk): TDEE + 300 kcal/day (lean bulk approach)
  • Maintenance: No adjustment
Hall, K.D. (2008). "What is the required energy deficit per unit weight loss?" International Journal of Obesity, 32, 573–576.
doi:10.1038/sj.ijo.0803720
Iraki, J., Fitschen, P., Espinar, S., & Helms, E. (2019). "Nutrition Recommendations for Bodybuilders in the Off-Season: A Narrative Review." Sports, 7(7), 154.
doi:10.3390/sports7070154
Recommends a caloric surplus of 250–500 kcal for lean muscle gain.

4. Macronutrient Distribution

Protein Intake

GGFit recommends approximately 2.0 g of protein per kilogram of body weight, consistent with the International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) position stand for physically active individuals.

Jäger, R., Kerksick, C.M., Campbell, B.I., et al. (2017). "International Society of Sports Nutrition Position Stand: Protein and Exercise." Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition, 14, 20.
doi:10.1186/s12970-017-0177-8
Recommends 1.4–2.0 g/kg/day for exercising individuals.

Fat Intake

Fat intake is set at approximately 0.8 g per kilogram of body weight, consistent with recommendations for athletes to consume 20–35% of calories from fat.

Helms, E.R., Aragon, A.A., & Fitschen, P.J. (2014). "Evidence-based recommendations for natural bodybuilding contest preparation: nutrition and supplementation." Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition, 11, 20.
doi:10.1186/1550-2783-11-20

Carbohydrate Intake

Carbohydrate intake is calculated as the remaining calories after protein and fat requirements are met, divided by 4 kcal/g. This flexible approach is consistent with evidence-based guidelines that prioritize protein and fat adequacy first.

5. Muscle Recovery Estimates

GGFit estimates a 48-hour recovery window per muscle group after resistance training, based on research on muscle protein synthesis (MPS) timelines.

Damas, F., Phillips, S., Vechin, F.C., & Ugrinowitsch, C. (2015). "A review of resistance training-induced changes in skeletal muscle protein synthesis and their contribution to hypertrophy." Sports Medicine, 45(6), 801–807.
doi:10.1007/s40279-015-0320-0
MPS elevation typically returns to baseline within 24–48 hours post-exercise in trained individuals.
